Introduction

Project Background

The project focused on designing and modelling a functional soap dispenser mechanism inspired by the skull structure of a Xenomorph creature from the Alien franchise whilst still being creatively distinct. The main problem being solved was the lack of engaging and visually distinctive household products that combine functionality with creative design. Traditional soap dispensers are often generic in appearance and provide little interaction or aesthetic value beyond their basic purpose. This project aimed to create a dispenser that was both practical and visually impactful while exploring the integration of mechanical systems into a product.

A themed soap dispenser was chosen to make the product more unique and appealing to a specific audience, particularly fans of science fiction and cinematic creature design. Incorporating the dispensing mechanism into such an unconventional structure created an opportunity to blend artistic modelling with engineering considerations. The project also encouraged creative problem-solving, as the mechanism needed to function efficiently while remaining concealed within the sculpted design.

This concept was relevant because themed and personalised products are increasingly popular within modern consumer markets. Many people seek decorative items that reflect personal interests, especially in entertainment and pop culture. At the same time, there is growing interest in interactive and novelty household objects that enhance user experience. By combining a recognisable fictional design with a practical everyday object, the project explored how product design can improve user engagement while maintaining usability and ergonomic considerations.

The task was selected as a robotic and mechanical modelling project because it required the integration of moving components and functional mechanisms within a detailed 3D structure. Designing the soap dispenser involved considering motion, force application, component placement, and the interaction between internal mechanical parts and the external shell. The project also allowed the use of CAD modelling, prototyping techniques, and mechanical analysis, making it an effective challenge for developing skills in both creative modelling and engineering design.

Aim

The aim of this project was to develop an automatic soap dispenser that had a sci-fi alien theme, that could ultimately become commercially available.

Objectives

  • One objective of the project was to create 2D concept designs for the Alien-themed soap dispenser. These designs were used to explore different ideas and choose the most suitable concept.
  • Another objective was to develop a 3D CAD model of the dispenser and its mechanism. This allowed the design to be planned accurately before prototyping.
  • The project also aimed to produce a physical prototype using 3D printing and simulation techniques. This helped test the appearance and functionality of the design.
  • A further objective was to design a supporting base structure for the dispenser. The base needed to provide stability and match the overall theme of the product.
  • The final objective was to evaluate the prototyping techniques used during the project. This included reviewing the effectiveness of the modelling and manufacturing methods.

    • Scope and Restraints

    The project faced several time limitations which affected the overall progress and completion of the artefact. Modelling, testing, and printing individual components required significant time, and delays during the prototyping stage reduced the opportunity for further refinement and assembly. As a result, the final prototype could not be fully printed in time. However, that being said all the models are there and a simulation has been created to account for this.

    Printer size limitations also impacted the design and manufacturing process. The dimensions of the 3D printer restricted the maximum size of individual parts, meaning larger components had to be divided into sections before printing. This increased assembly time, increased cost for the university printers, and introduced additional challenges when aligning and joining the printed pieces together.

    Material limitations affected both the strength and finish of the prototype. The available printing materials were suitable for producing a visual model, but they had limitations in durability and detail accuracy. Some parts required redesigning to suit the material properties and prevent structural weakness during printing.

    The artefact remained incomplete due to the combination of time constraints, printing difficulties, and assembly issues. While the main structure and concept were successfully developed, some functional and cosmetic elements could not be fully implemented before submission. Despite this, the project still demonstrates the intended design process and technical development.

    Group coordination challenges also influenced the progress of the project. Organising workloads, managing communication, and coordinating access to equipment required careful planning. These coordination issues largely affected print times for the main skull and delayed the designing of the necessary mechanisms.

    Budget and workshop access were additional limitations throughout the project. Restricted funding limited the quantity of materials and prototype iterations that could be produced in the 3D printing lab. In addition, workshop and printer availability had to be shared with other users, reducing the amount of time available for manufacturing and testing components.
