Soapamorph

Max McGill

Introduction

Project Background

The project focused on designing and modelling a functional soap dispenser inspired by the skull structure of an alien creature from the Alien franchise. The aim was to create a visually distinctive product that combines functionality with creative design, improving on generic household soap dispensers.

A themed soap dispenser was chosen to appeal to fans of sci-fi and creature design, allowing artistic modelling to be combined with mechanical engineering. The mechanism needed to be functional while remaining hidden within the design.

This concept reflects growing demand for personalised and novelty household products linked to pop culture. It explores how design can improve user engagement while remaining practical.

The project required integration of mechanical systems within a detailed 3D structure, involving motion, force, and component placement. It provided experience in CAD modelling, prototyping, and mechanical design.

Aim

The aim of this project was to develop an automatic soap dispenser that had a sci-fi alien theme, that could ultimately become commercially available.

Objectives

  • One objective of the project was to create 2D concept designs for the Alien-themed soap dispenser. These designs were used to explore different ideas and choose the most suitable concept.
  • Another objective was to develop a 3D CAD model of the dispenser and its mechanism. This allowed the design to be planned accurately before prototyping.
  • The project also aimed to produce a physical prototype using 3D printing and simulation techniques. This helped test the appearance and functionality of the design.
  • A further objective was to design a supporting base structure for the dispenser. The base needed to provide stability and match the overall theme of the product.
  • The final objective was to evaluate the prototyping techniques used during the project. This included reviewing the effectiveness of the modelling and manufacturing methods.

    • Scope and Restraints

    Time limitations restricted modelling, testing, and printing, preventing full completion. A simulation was created to represent the final mechanism.

    Printer size constraints meant parts had to be split, increasing assembly time and complexity.

    Material limitations affected strength and detail, requiring redesigns to suit printability.

    The artefact remained incomplete due to time, printing, and assembly issues, although the main concept was successfully developed.

    Group coordination issues slowed print scheduling and mechanism development due to communication and timing conflicts.

    Budget and workshop access limited material use and reduced available printing and testing time.

    Product Development

    Concept

    Once the group had agreed on a general concept for the artefact, the method of mounting and support needed to be carefully considered. This led to an extended discussion, as some members preferred fixing the dispenser to a back panel, while others felt a freestanding stand would be more effective. In the end, a hybrid approach combining both methods was adopted to provide stability and practicality.

    The initial drawings included a relatively large base designed to store the soap supply used by the dispenser, along with space for additional refill storage. Although this offered practical benefits, the overall size was too large to realistically fit on a standard sink area, making it impractical for everyday use.

    As part of the functional development, the group also considered how the soap would be dispensed. A key requirement was that the system should ideally be hands-free to improve hygiene and user experience. This led to discussions around using a sensor-based mechanism, such as infrared or ultrasonic detection, or a pressure-activated system to trigger the pump without direct contact. These ideas were evaluated in terms of reliability, complexity, and ease of integration into the design. For this project, the ultrasonic sensors were selected for their ease of use.

    Alongside this, different pump mechanisms were also explored. The group considered simple manual pumps, spring-loaded systems, and small motor-driven peristaltic pumps. Each option was assessed based on cost, space requirements, and how effectively it could be concealed within the Alien-skull design. Ultimately, the chosen method was the peristaltic pump since this offered the best balance of functionality and aesthetic integration.

    A mechanical system for the jaw and tongue would use components such as levers, linkages, springs, or cams, offering a simpler and more reliable solution that is easier to construct and less dependent on power or programming. However, it would likely provide less precise control over movement and could be challenging to fit neatly within the limited internal space of the Alien-skull design. In contrast, an electronic system using servos, motors, or linear actuators controlled by a microcontroller would allow for smoother, more accurate, and potentially synchronised movements, improving the realism of the mechanism. By using a combination of both methods, an accurate yet reliable solution could be achieved. This mechanism would use a mechanical actuator to map the jaw rotation to the tongue extension and a motor to drive the rotation of the jaw.

    Following these considerations, the group moved away from the initial large-scale concept drawings and began developing a scaled physical model. This allowed the design to be better understood in terms of real-world proportions and helped identify potential issues earlier in the development process before progressing to a final prototype. (Blender Foundation, 2025)

    Prototyping

    2D Design

    The initial drawings played an important role in establishing the overall form, proportions, and aesthetic direction of the alien-skull themed soap dispenser. Early sketches focused on defining key features such as the skull shape, jaw position, and general silhouette of the artefact. These drawings also helped determine approximate dimensions, ensuring the design would remain realistic and usable on a standard sink area. Layout planning was used to organise where internal components, such as the soap reservoir and actuation mechanism, would be positioned within the structure. Working in 2D before moving into CAD was beneficial because it allowed ideas to be developed quickly, tested visually, and refined without the time required for detailed digital modelling.

    CAD Designs

    The CAD development process involved two main software packages: Blender and Fusion 360. The alien-skull base model was first created through 3D scanning and then imported into Blender, where it was cleaned up, modified, and adapted for the project requirements. Blender, supported by a measurement extension, was used to refine the skull geometry, adjust proportions, and modify the jaw and tongue areas so they could accommodate the internal mechanism. This approach allowed the group to use a highly detailed and realistic skull shape while still being able to customise it for functionality. (Autodesk, 2025).

    Fusion 360 was used separately to design the internal mechanical system responsible for actuating the jaw and tongue movement. This included the development of moving components, mounting points, and the overall layout of the mechanism within the skull structure. The modelling workflow involved repeatedly testing how the scanned skull and mechanical components would fit together, leading to several iterative revisions throughout development.“Iterative design is widely used in engineering development (Cross, 2011).”(Autodesk, 2025)

    Scaling issues became an important consideration, as the scanned skull model and the mechanism created in Fusion 360 needed to match accurately in size and proportion. Tolerances also had to be considered carefully to ensure moving parts would function correctly once manufactured, particularly due to the inaccuracies that can occur during 3D printing. In addition, the final model was divided into multiple sections to fit within the limitations of the available 3D printer build volume and to make assembly easier during prototyping.

    Physical Prototyping

    3D Printing

    The initial 3D printing stage revealed several issues with the prototype models. A number of prints failed due to poor geometry within some of the CAD files, which created problems during slicing and caused unstable sections during printing. Scaling issues also became apparent, as the original alien-skull model was significantly larger than the available printer build volume. This meant the design had to be divided into multiple separate sections before printing could begin. Support generation also caused difficulties, particularly around complex curved surfaces and overhanging areas of the skull and jaw, resulting in rough surface finishes and increased post-processing requirements. In addition, the available printer had limited build capacity, which restricted the overall size and quality of the prototype components.

    Following the issues identified during the initial printing stage, several revisions were made to improve manufacturability. Access to a larger 3D printer allowed larger sections of the model to be produced with fewer joins, improving structural consistency and reducing assembly complexity. The model was also redesigned into more manageable separated sections, allowing components to be printed more reliably and efficiently. Tolerances between moving parts and assembly sections were adjusted to improve fit and reduce friction during operation. These revisions resulted in a more practical and manufacturable prototype compared to the original print attempts.(Gibson et al., 2015)

    The prototype was printed using PLA filament, primarily because it was the only material readily available for use during the project. While PLA was suitable for rapid prototyping due to its ease of printing and low cost, polypropylene (PP) would likely have been a more appropriate material for a final functional product because of its improved durability, flexibility, and resistance to moisture and chemicals commonly found in bathroom environments.

    Construction

    Wood was selected as the primary material for the base because it was inexpensive, easy to manufacture, and provided sufficient strength and stability for supporting the prototype. It also allowed modifications to be made quickly using standard workshop tools, making it suitable for a prototype environment. Although laser cutting was considered as a manufacturing method due to its accuracy and cleaner finish, it was ultimately rejected because of time and budget limitations. Producing the components through laser cutting would have increased manufacturing costs and required additional setup time that was not available within the project schedule.

    Prototype Evaluation

    Although the prototype remained incomplete, it was still possible to evaluate several aspects of the design. Structurally, the artefact demonstrated sufficient integrity to support its main components and validate the overall assembly approach. The design also showed practicality in terms of layout and mechanism integration, despite some unfinished functional elements. Visually, the alien-skull appearance successfully achieved the intended aesthetic and demonstrated the effectiveness of combining detailed organic forms with mechanical components. From a manufacturing perspective, the project highlighted both the strengths and limitations of the chosen prototyping methods, identifying areas where tolerances, material selection, and assembly techniques could be improved in future development.

    Simulation

    A digital simulation was developed to demonstrate the intended mechanical functionality of the design. The simulation was used to showcase the movement of the jaw and tongue actuation system, allowing the group to present how the mechanism would operate within the completed alien-skull soap dispenser. This approach provided a practical alternative to a fully assembled prototype, as it allowed the key mechanical concepts and movement sequences to be tested and visualised without requiring final physical manufacture. The simulation also demonstrated the integration between the structural design and the internal mechanism, helping validate the overall functionality of the project despite the incomplete physical artefact.

    Project Management

    Team Roles

    The project was completed through a collaborative workflow in which different members focused on specific areas of development, including the alien-skull structure, the mechanical actuation system, and the electronics integration. Communication between team members was carried out through Discord and in-person meetings, allowing design updates, problem solving, and progress discussions to take place regularly throughout the project. Meeting logs and development updates were also uploaded to Blackboard to maintain records of decisions and project progression.

    There was a strong dependency between different areas of the project, meaning each stage relied heavily on the progress and design choices of other team members. The size and shape of the mechanical actuation system were entirely dependent on the creative and structural decisions made during the development of the alien-skull model. Similarly, the electronics integration depended on both the final mechanism design and the available internal space created within the skull structure. This required continuous communication and coordination between members to ensure all components could function together within the limitations of the overall design.

    Time Management

    Project planning was managed using a Gantt chart to organise the development stages and track overall progress throughout the project. The schedule helped structure tasks such as concept development, CAD modelling, prototyping, mechanism design, and assembly, allowing the group to coordinate workloads and identify dependencies between different stages of development.

    Throughout the project, several delays affected progress and prevented the artefact from being fully completed within the intended timeframe. One of the main issues involved 3D printing bottlenecks, as printer availability, limited build volume, and failed prints significantly slowed manufacturing. Large components had to be separated into multiple sections, increasing print times and assembly requirements. In some cases, failed prints also required models to be adjusted and reprinted, further delaying progress.

    Additional delays were caused by redesign work during the CAD and prototyping stages. As the alien-skull structure and mechanical system were developed separately, revisions were often needed to ensure both systems aligned correctly and could fit within the available internal space. Problems relating to tolerances, geometry, and movement constraints resulted in repeated modifications to both the structural and mechanical components before a workable solution could be achieved.

    Weekly meetings were held to review progress, address issues, and update the plan based on development changes. These meetings allowed the group to track progress against the Gantt chart, redistribute tasks where necessary, and respond to emerging problems such as design conflicts or manufacturing limitations. Meeting discussions were recorded and logged on Blackboard, ensuring that decisions and changes were properly documented. These documents were datestamped for future reference.

    Problem Solving

    Critical Analysis of Prototyping Techniques

    CAD Software

    CAD provided precise modelling, accurate dimensions, and fast design changes, making it highly effective for development. It is widely used in industry for product design. However, it isn't as good for creative design processes.

    3D Printing

    3D printing allowed fast and low-cost prototyping, enabling physical testing of parts. However, it was limited by long print times, surface quality issues, and material waste from failed prints. The project also required splitting models due to printer size limits which increased assembly complexity.(Ultimaker, 2024)

    Laser Cutting

    Laser cutting was considered for the base due to its precision and speed but was rejected due to time and budget constraints. It would have been more suitable for flat components like the base rather than the complex 3D skull structure.(Kalpakjian and Schmid, 2014)

    Scanning/Sculpting

    3D scanning was used to create the base skull model, while motion capture and haptic sculpting were not used due to relevance. These technologies are mainly used in animation, advanced design, or medical/industrial applications rather than small-scale prototyping.(Revopoint, 2024)

    Comparison

    The combination of CAD, scanning, and 3D printing provided the best balance of flexibility, cost, and speed. While industrial methods offer better quality and scalability, they are less suitable for early-stage prototypes. This workflow allowed effective development despite limitations in manufacturing and finishing quality.

    Reflection and Improvements

    What went well

    The project showed strong use of iterative design throughout all stages of development. Initial sketches were gradually refined into more detailed CAD models, with multiple revisions made to improve fit, functionality, and overall design quality. This cycle of testing and refinement was particularly effective in identifying issues early, especially when integrating the mechanical system with the alien-skull structure.

    Teamwork was another key strength of the project. Responsibilities were clearly divided between members, which allowed parallel development of the skull model, mechanical system, and supporting elements. Regular communication helped ensure that changes in one area were reflected in others, reducing major conflicts between components.

    The group also demonstrated adaptability when facing unexpected issues such as printing failures, scaling problems, and design constraints. Instead of sticking rigidly to the original plan, the design was adjusted through practical problem-solving, including splitting components for printing and revising tolerances where necessary.

    What went badly

    Despite strong design development, several issues negatively impacted the final outcome. One of the main problems was late printing, which created a bottleneck during the final stages of production. Because prints took longer than expected and some failed, this reduced the time available for assembly and refinement.

    As a result, the artefact remained incomplete within the project deadline. Although most components were designed and tested virtually, the lack of a fully assembled physical model limited the ability to fully evaluate performance, durability, and real-world usability.

    What would be Improved

    In future projects, earlier prototyping would be prioritised to identify physical constraints sooner. Producing test prints at an earlier stage would help highlight issues such as scaling errors, weak geometry, and tolerance problems before final modelling decisions are locked in.

    More structured tolerance testing would also improve the reliability of moving parts, particularly in the jaw and tongue mechanism. This would reduce the need for redesigns during later stages of development.

    Stronger project planning and time allocation would help reduce delays, particularly in relation to 3D printing queues and iterative revisions. Additionally, exploring alternative manufacturing methods such as resin printing, CNC machining, or hybrid fabrication approaches could improve both accuracy and surface finish while reducing reliance on a single process.

    Future Development

    Further development could include waterproofing the system for real-world bathroom use and integrating electronics more effectively into the mechanism. Sensor-based activation could improve usability and hygiene, while higher-grade production materials would increase durability. In the long term, the design could be refined into a commercially manufacturable product using scalable manufacturing processes. (BASF, 2023; Adafruit Industries, 2023; Ultimaker, 2024)

    Conclusion

    Final Thoughts

    The project successfully developed a detailed concept for an alien themed soap dispenser, combining both mechanical engineering and creative product design. The final outcome demonstrates a strong integration of aesthetic design with functional mechanism development, even though the artefact was not fully physically completed within the timeframe.

    A key strength of the project was the combination of virtual and physical prototyping. CAD modelling, 3D scanning, and simulation tools allowed rapid development and testing of ideas before committing to manufacture, while 3D printing provided valuable real-world validation of parts and assemblies. This hybrid workflow helped reduce design uncertainty and improved overall decision-making during development.

    Several important lessons were learned, particularly around time management, manufacturing constraints, and the importance of early physical testing. The experience highlighted how design decisions in CAD directly impact manufacturability and assembly.

    Overall, the methods used were effective for a prototype-stage project, providing a strong foundation for future refinement. While limitations in time and manufacturing prevented full completion, the project successfully demonstrated the design process from concept to near-functional prototype and identified clear directions for future improvement.
